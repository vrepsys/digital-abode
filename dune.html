Dune by Frank Herbert

There's no arguing that Dune is a seminal science fiction novel that made a great influence on the whole genre. Naturally, I had high expectations. High expectations lead to big disappointments — and this is exactly what happened to me with Dune. It just didn't resonate with me.

To me Dune is Game of Thrones, but with spaceships and laser guns. I found it to be closer to the fantasy genre than science fiction. I think if you're a big fan of Star Wars and Lord of the Rings there's a high chance you'll enjoy Dune as well.

I found it unconvincing, for example, that people can travel space, but they solve conflicts by having knife fights, and then troubadours singing songs about it.

I also wish characters in the book had more color. Too black and white for my taste. The good guys are very good, endlessly honourable, kind, and also good looking. The bad guys have no honour, look ugly, and are fat.

It was interesting to learn how Frank Herbert imagined the beginning of space travel. Remember the book was first published in 1965 — just 4 years after the first man in space, and before the Moon landing.

The first space experiences, poorly communicated and subject to extreme distortion, were a wild inducement to mystical speculation.

Immediately, space gave a different flavor and sense to ideas of Creation. That difference is seen even in the highest religious achievements of the period. All through religion, the feeling of the sacred was touched by anarchy from the outer dark.

In 2021, when a lot of people had already been to space, we know that these experiences didn't significantly change neither the humanity nor religion. Not yet at least. I wonder how things are going to change when space travel is going to become widely available.

Even if it wasn't my favourite book, I feel like it was still worth reading. I appreciate learning a little bit more about the history of science fiction. I also really look forward to seeing the movie now!
